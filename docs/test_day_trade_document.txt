DAY TRADING OVERVIEW

DEFINITION
Day trading is the practice of opening and closing positions within the same
trading day. A day trader typically avoids holding positions overnight in
order to reduce exposure to after-hours news, earnings releases, and gap risk.

COMMON MARKETS
Day traders may trade stocks, futures, foreign exchange, options, or
cryptocurrencies. Each market has different trading hours, liquidity
conditions, margin rules, and volatility patterns.

CORE GOAL
The main goal of day trading is to capture small price movements repeatedly
while managing downside risk carefully. Successful day trading depends more on
discipline, process, and risk control than on prediction.

COMMON STRATEGIES
Momentum trading:
Momentum traders look for strong price movement supported by volume. They may
enter when a stock breaks above a key level and exits quickly if the move
fails.

Breakout trading:
Breakout traders watch consolidation ranges and key resistance or support
levels. A trade is taken when price moves out of the range with confirmation.

Reversal trading:
Reversal traders look for exhaustion at extremes, such as failed breakouts,
climactic candles, or overextended moves into major levels.

Scalping:
Scalpers take many small trades and usually hold positions for seconds or
minutes. This strategy requires fast execution and strict attention to fees
and slippage.

KEY RISKS
Leverage risk:
Leverage can amplify gains but also magnifies losses. A trader using leverage
must define risk before entering the trade.

Gap risk:
Although day traders usually close positions before the session ends, fast
news events can still cause sudden adverse movement during open market hours.

Emotional risk:
Fear, overconfidence, and revenge trading can damage decision quality.
Emotional instability is a common reason for inconsistent performance.

Execution risk:
A trader may not get the expected entry or exit price because of slippage,
latency, or thin liquidity.

RISK MANAGEMENT PRINCIPLES
Rule 1:
Risk a small fixed percentage of account equity per trade. A common risk model
is to risk one percent or less of total account equity on a single trade.

Rule 2:
Always define the stop loss before entering a position. The stop loss marks
the price level where the trade idea is considered invalid.

Rule 3:
Use position sizing instead of hope. Position size should be calculated from
account size, stop distance, and maximum acceptable loss.

Rule 4:
Do not force trades during low-quality market conditions. Capital preservation
is part of the trading process.

SAMPLE POSITION SIZE EXAMPLE
If a trader has a ten-thousand-dollar account and is willing to risk one
percent per trade, the maximum loss is one hundred dollars.
If the stop loss is fifty cents away from entry, the position size is two
hundred shares because one hundred dollars divided by fifty cents equals two
hundred.

TOOLS A DAY TRADER MAY USE
- price charts
- volume indicators
- watchlists
- market scanners
- news feeds
- economic calendars
- broker order-entry platform
- trading journal

IMPORTANT METRICS
Win rate:
The percentage of trades that close profitably.

Risk-reward ratio:
The expected gain compared with the planned loss on a trade.

Maximum drawdown:
The largest peak-to-trough decline in account value over a period.

Expectancy:
The average amount a trading system is expected to gain or lose per trade over
time.

SAMPLE DAILY ROUTINE
Pre-market:
Review overnight news, identify catalysts, mark key price levels, and define
the watchlist.

Market open:
Observe volatility carefully. Many day traders reduce size at the open because
price movement can be fast and unpredictable.

Midday:
Liquidity and momentum may slow down. Some traders stop trading during the
midday session unless a high-conviction setup appears.

Close:
Review all trades, document mistakes, and record lessons in a journal.

COMMON MISTAKES
- trading without a plan
- moving a stop loss farther away after entry
- increasing size after losses to recover quickly
- ignoring transaction costs
- trading illiquid instruments
- taking low-quality setups out of boredom

PSYCHOLOGY AND PROCESS
Consistency usually comes from repeatable execution rather than from constant
strategy switching. A trader should track setups, results, and emotional state
to identify patterns in decision-making.

FINAL NOTE
Day trading is high risk. A beginner should practice with simulation, study
market structure, and focus on risk management before committing real capital.
